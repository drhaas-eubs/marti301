Why Most AI Strategies Fail Before They Start

Understanding AI Maturity as a Prerequisite for Strategic Investment
Key Framework: AI Maturity Staircase

The enthusiasm gap in artificial intelligence is well documented. A 2025 McKinsey survey of 1,993 executives found that while 80% of organisations report using AI in at least one business function, only 6% qualify as 'high performers' — those attributing more than 5% of EBIT to AI (McKinsey & Company, 2025). The question is not whether firms are adopting AI, but why so few are extracting meaningful value from it.

One explanation lies in what practitioners call the AI maturity gap. Organisations at different stages of AI capability require fundamentally different strategies, governance structures, and investment profiles. Applying an enterprise AI strategy to an organisation that has not yet mastered basic data hygiene is like asking a first-year medical student to perform surgery — the ambition exceeds the capability.

The AI Maturity Staircase provides a diagnostic framework for this problem. Originally adapted from Davenport and Harris's (2017) analytics maturity model, it classifies organisations across four stages. At the Ad Hoc stage, AI initiatives are isolated experiments driven by individual enthusiasts, with no central coordination or governance. Data exists in silos, and there is no repeatable process for moving from prototype to production. At the Experimental stage, the organisation runs multiple pilots with some executive sponsorship, but lacks the infrastructure to scale successful experiments. This is where most firms stall — the 'pilot purgatory' identified by VentureBeat (2019), where 87% of AI projects never reach production.

The Operational stage marks a qualitative shift. Here, AI systems run in production, integrated into business processes with monitoring, retraining schedules, and clear ownership. Governance structures exist — model risk taxonomies, bias audits, human-in-the-loop protocols — and AI investment is tied to measurable business outcomes. Finally, the Transformational stage describes organisations where AI is not a department or a project, but an operating principle. Business models are designed around AI capabilities, and the organisation continuously learns from its AI systems.

The strategic implication is that investment should match maturity. An Ad Hoc organisation investing in enterprise-wide automation will fail — not because the technology is wrong, but because the organisational substrate cannot support it. Conversely, a Transformational organisation that still treats AI as a series of isolated projects is leaving value on the table.

McKinsey's 2025 data reinforces this: high-performing organisations are three times more likely to report strong senior leadership commitment to AI, and nearly 80% pursue innovation — not just efficiency — as an AI objective (McKinsey & Company, 2025). The staircase is not merely descriptive; it is prescriptive. Know where you stand before deciding where to go.
